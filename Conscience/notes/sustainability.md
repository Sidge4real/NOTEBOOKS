**Sustainability and Energy**

## 1. Sustainable Development Goals (SDGs)
The United Nations established 17 Sustainable Development Goals in 2015.  
They form a global framework to address poverty, inequality, health, education, and environmental protection by 2030.

Relevant goals for the energy and digital sector include:

- Affordable and Clean Energy (SDG 7)
- Responsible Consumption and Production (SDG 12)
- Climate Action (SDG 13)

These goals guide governments, organisations, and individuals toward a more sustainable and resilient future.

## 2. Carbon Literacy
Carbon Literacy refers to the knowledge, motivation, and skills needed to take meaningful climate action.  
It focuses on understanding:

- the carbon impact of daily activities,
- the urgency of climate change,
- how to reduce emissions at individual, organisational, and community levels.

The Carbon Literacy Project is a large international initiative recognised by the UN for its impact on climate education and behaviour change.

## 3. Digital Carbon Footprint
Digital activities contribute significantly to global emissions. Key insights include:

- The average internet user spends 3,230 hours online per year, representing about 40% of their recommended carbon budget.
- AI systems are increasingly energy‑intensive and are becoming default features on many devices.
- Data centres, AI workloads, and cryptocurrency mining are major contributors to rising electricity demand.

Examples of digital carbon impact:

- A spam email: 0.03 g CO2
- A long email: 17 g CO2
- A Google search: 0.5 g CO2
- One hour of video conferencing: 50 g CO2
- Producing a new laptop has a footprint comparable to a flight from London to Rome

Global ICT is responsible for an estimated 2–4% of worldwide emissions.

## 4. Why Action Is Needed
The digital sector emits roughly the same amount of CO2 as the aviation industry.  
AI adoption is accelerating energy demand, forcing companies to reconsider their climate strategies.

Every gram of greenhouse gas avoided reduces the risk of climate‑related damage.  
Climate change is no longer a future scenario; it is a present and escalating reality.

## 5. Digital Activities in a Carbon Context
Different digital actions have different carbon impacts. Examples include:

- Email, search queries, and video calls each contribute measurable emissions.
- Mobile phone usage generates hundreds of millions of tonnes of CO2 annually, mostly from production.
- Websites with heavy media content increase energy use for both servers and users.

Extending device lifetimes, reducing unnecessary digital consumption, and choosing greener hosting options can significantly reduce emissions.

## 6. Mitigation and Adaptation
Mitigation refers to reducing greenhouse gas emissions.  
Adaptation refers to preparing for and adjusting to the effects of climate change.

Examples:

- Mitigation: solar energy, energy‑efficient software, green hosting, reducing hardware waste.
- Adaptation: flood prediction systems, high‑accuracy climate models, resilient infrastructure.

Both approaches are necessary to address the climate crisis.

## 7. Low‑Carbon Strategies for the Digital and Tech Sector
Organisations can reduce their digital carbon footprint through several strategies:

- Hosting services on low‑carbon or renewable‑powered data centres.
- Repairing, reusing, and recycling hardware instead of discarding it.
- Reducing rich media on websites to lower energy consumption.
- Using smart meters and sustainable technologies at home and in the workplace.
- Tracking employee and organisational emissions to identify reduction opportunities.

## 8. Circular Economy Principles
A circular economy aims to eliminate waste and keep materials in use for as long as possible.  
Key principles include:

- Designing out waste and pollution.
- Keeping products and materials in circulation.
- Regenerating natural systems.

This approach is especially relevant for hardware, where production has a high carbon cost.

## 9. Carbon Footprints, Handprints, and Shadows
- A carbon footprint measures total emissions caused by an individual, organisation, or product.
- A carbon handprint measures positive climate actions taken.
- A climate shadow includes indirect impacts, such as investments, consumption habits, and lifestyle choices.

Balancing footprint and handprint is essential for achieving carbon neutrality.

## 10. Greenwashing Awareness
Greenwashing occurs when organisations present themselves as more environmentally friendly than they actually are.  
To identify greenwashing:

- Be cautious with imagery and colour use.
- Look for misleading claims or numbers.
- Check for real certifications and accreditations.
- Evaluate whether the organisation’s broader actions match its sustainability claims.

## 11. Global and European Climate Policy
Key frameworks include:

- The Paris Agreement, aiming to limit global warming to well below 2°C.
- COP conferences, where global climate negotiations take place.
- The EU’s 2050 climate‑neutrality strategy.
- The European Climate Pact, which encourages citizen and organisational participation.
- The European Green Digital Coalition, promoting sustainable digital transformation.

These policies shape the direction of climate action in the digital and energy sectors.

## 12. Net‑Zero and Carbon Management
Achieving net‑zero requires:

- Reducing emissions as much as possible.
- Removing remaining emissions through natural or technological means.
- Using carbon offsets only when reduction is not possible.

The carbon management hierarchy prioritises:

1. Avoiding emissions  
2. Reducing emissions  
3. Replacing high‑carbon processes  
4. Offsetting as a last resort

## 13. Hardware and Embedded Carbon
Hardware production has a significant carbon footprint due to materials, manufacturing, and transport.  
Key considerations:

- Extend device lifetimes.
- Repair instead of replace.
- Recycle components responsibly.
- Encourage sustainable procurement policies.

## 14. Digitalisation as a Climate Solution
Digital technology can also reduce emissions when used responsibly. Examples include:

- AI‑driven energy optimisation.
- Remote work reducing travel emissions.
- Digital twins improving industrial efficiency.
- Smart agriculture and resource monitoring.

The challenge is ensuring digitalisation saves more energy than it consumes.
