# Field Observations — Digital Inclusion Exercises

## 1. Digihelper Shift (12 hours, City of Antwerp)

### Context
During a 12‑hour shift as a digihelper, I assisted citizens with digital questions and guided them step by step through online tasks.

### Key Observations
- Most people understand the general structure of apps and websites.
- The main barrier has shifted from “I don’t know how to do this” to “I am afraid to do this.”
- Fear is driven by:
  
    - phishing attempts,
    - hacking stories,
    - rising digital crime,
    - general distrust of online systems.

- Many users hesitate before clicking anything, even when they know the correct action.
- People frequently ask for reassurance that a website or action is safe.
- Confidence, not skill, is often the real issue.

### Younger vs. Older Users (based on conversations and reports)
- Younger people often come in for help with the itsme setup process, which they find difficult to configure but easy to use afterward.
- Older people also struggle with itsme, but they find both setup and usage difficult.
- Younger users tend to trust digital tools more easily, sometimes too easily.
- Older users are more cautious and more likely to question suspicious behaviour online.

### Interaction Preferences
- Younger people prefer using a mouse and cursor; it feels natural to them.
- Older people prefer using their finger (touch interaction) because it feels more intuitive and direct.
- Older users struggle more with precise cursor control.

### Implications
Digital support is increasingly about:

- reducing fear,
- building trust,
- validating that users are doing the right thing,
- helping people feel safe online.

## 2. Usability Observation Session (3 participants)

### Method
Three participants were asked to complete tasks on a website without receiving help.  
I observed their behaviour, navigation patterns, and reactions.

### Observed Behaviour
- Participants relied heavily on visual cues rather than reading text.
- They often scrolled past important information without noticing it.
- They struggled to distinguish between:
  - navigation elements and content,
  - primary and secondary actions.
- When unsure, they tended to freeze rather than explore alternatives.
- None of the participants used search functions, even when available.

### Age‑Related Differences (observed)
- Younger participants clicked faster and explored more quickly.
- This behaviour is both positive (faster discovery) and negative (higher risk of clicking the wrong thing).
- Older participants took more time and were more cautious.
- Older users were less comfortable with the mouse and cursor movement.
- Older participants showed more signs of low basic IT skills.
- Few younger people participated, so conclusions are limited.

### Emotional Responses
- Frustration when encountering unclear layouts.
- Relief when finding the correct path, even by accident.
- Increased anxiety when asked to enter personal information.

### Key Insight
The challenge is not only digital literacy but also cognitive load.  
Users become overwhelmed when:

- the interface is cluttered,
- terminology is unclear,
- steps are unpredictable,
- feedback is missing or ambiguous.

---

## Overall Conclusions

### 1. Fear is becoming a dominant barrier
People increasingly fear making mistakes online, even when they know the correct steps.

### 2. Trust is as important as usability
A usable interface is not enough; users must feel safe and reassured.

### 3. Interfaces overload users quickly
Small design issues significantly increase confusion and hesitation.

### 4. Digital inclusion requires emotional support
Helping people participate digitally means:

- reducing anxiety,
- building confidence,
- simplifying choices,
- validating their actions.

### 5. Skills alone are not the issue
Most users know the basics of how digital tools work.  
They struggle with:

- uncertainty,
- fear of scams,
- lack of trust,
- overwhelming interfaces,
- unfamiliar interaction methods (especially older users),
- and difficulty recognising when something is unsafe (especially younger users).
